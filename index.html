Productivity went up. Then cooperation fell apart.

A government agency rewarded individual output. Output went up. Then everything else went down.

A public-sector org rolled out performance-based pay. It worked at first. Then people stopped helping each other, started hoarding info, and morale tanked. Turns out money isn't the whole story — people also care about fairness, colleagues, and whether their work actually means something.

§ I · Theory

Four Ideas That Explain Everything

01 · Economics
Principal-Agent Theory

Bosses and employees want different things. Financial incentives try to bridge that gap. On paper, clean and logical. In practice, complicated fast.

Traditional Economics
02 · Psychology
The Crowding-Out Effect

Big external rewards can kill internal ones. Once people work just for the bonus, they stop working because they care. Loyalty and teamwork quietly disappear.

Behavioural Economics
03 · Sociology
Social Preferences

People aren't just walking calculators. Fairness, belonging, and wanting to help a teammate are real motivators. Team bonuses tap into that rather than making colleagues compete.

Choice Architecture and Nudging

§ IV · Governance and Welfare

So What Does This Actually Mean?

People's Wellbeing

Systems that only track output slowly eat away at the parts of work that make it worth doing. When trust goes, you get turnover, sick days, and work that technically gets done but stops being any good.

Governance

Nudge tools like smart defaults can improve both efficiency and wellbeing at once. And unlike mandates, they don't take away anyone's freedom to choose differently, which makes them easier to defend.

"The question is never whether to architect the choice environment. It already is. The question is whether to do it thoughtfully."

Thaler and Sunstein, Nudge (2008)

People Don't Think Like Economists

Most employees go with the default, copy colleagues, and follow their gut. Designing systems that assume everyone is a perfectly rational agent will keep producing surprises.

The Best Approach Uses Both

Financial incentives plus behavioural design. Clear structures give direction; smart defaults make cooperation the easier option. The goal isn't to trick anyone. It's just to make the good choice easy.

§ V · Real World

This Actually Happened. At Microsoft.

Big Tech Case
Microsoft
Stack Ranking Era · Early 2000s to 2013
What went wrong
The system that turned teammates into rivals

For over a decade, Microsoft ran a "stack ranking" system. Every team had to rank its members on a bell curve: top 20%, middle 70%, bottom 10%. The bottom group faced demotion or firing, regardless of their actual performance. Sounds rigorous, right?

It was a disaster. Since only a fixed number of people could be top-ranked, working alongside a talented colleague became a threat, not an asset. People started hoarding information and avoiding high-performers so they wouldn't look worse by comparison.

"People responsible for features will openly sabotage other people's efforts. One of the most valuable things I learned was to give the appearance of being courteous while withholding just enough information from colleagues to ensure they didn't get ahead of me."
Former Microsoft developer, Vanity Fair
Crowding-Out Effect Zero-Sum Competition Social Preferences Destroyed
What they did about it
Satya Nadella scrapped it and built something very different

In 2013, Microsoft abolished stack ranking entirely. Under new CEO Satya Nadella, the company shifted to continuous feedback, team-based goals, and a culture built around a "growth mindset." No more forced curves, no more rank-and-yank.

The turnaround is remarkable. Employee satisfaction jumped from a Glassdoor score of 3.7 in 2013 to 4.4 by 2023. Microsoft Azure became a cloud giant. Innovation returned. The company went from a "lost decade" to a multi-trillion dollar resurgence.

It's a near-perfect real-world version of the case study: individual incentives crushed cooperation, and switching to collaborative structures brought it back.

§ VI · Proposed Solutions

Two Ideas That Could Actually Fix This

So now we know what goes wrong and why. The real question is: what do you actually do about it? Here are two practical solutions that go beyond tweaking the bonus structure and address the root problem — that people need to feel like they're part of something, not just competing within it.

01
Solution One
A Game-Based Work System

The idea here is simple: take the things that make games fun — points, levels, challenges, team quests — and wire them into how everyday work gets recognised. Not to make work feel like a video game, but to make effort visible, collaboration rewarded, and progress something people can actually see and feel.

In a traditional incentive system, if you quietly help a colleague solve a problem, nobody tracks that. It doesn't show up in your review. In a game-based system, it does. You earn points for sharing knowledge, mentoring a new joiner, or finishing a cross-team project. Teams accumulate points together and unlock shared rewards — an extra day off, a team lunch, priority on new projects.

The key difference from regular bonuses is that the rewards are transparent, frequent, and tied to behaviours the organisation actually wants — not just the output that's easiest to measure. It also taps into something behavioural economics knows well: people respond to immediate, visible feedback far more than to an annual review that feels abstract and far away.

Crucially, the scoring system is designed around team contributions, not individual rankings. There's no bell curve. Nobody loses points because a colleague did well. The game is designed to be cooperative by default, which is exactly the kind of choice architecture Thaler and Sunstein would approve of.

How it works in practice
Employees earn points for collaboration, knowledge sharing, and hitting team milestones
Points are visible to everyone, making contributions transparent across the organisation
Teams level up together and unlock shared perks, not individual prize money
Monthly challenges rotate to keep things fresh and prevent gaming the system
No forced rankings. No zero-sum competition. Everyone can win if the team wins.

02
Solution Two
A Weekly Sports Hour for Team Building

One hour a week. Everyone stops working and plays together. It sounds almost too simple, but the behavioural economics behind it is solid. Shared physical activity builds the kind of trust and familiarity that no team-building workshop on a Saturday ever quite manages — because it's regular, low-pressure, and genuinely fun.

The problem with most workplace relationships is that they're purely transactional. You only interact with colleagues when there's a task involved. That means your mental model of a coworker is built entirely on work outcomes: did they deliver, did they make your job harder, are they a rival or an ally? A weekly sports session breaks that frame entirely. You're just people playing a game together.

From a social preferences perspective, this matters a lot. Behavioural economists know that people treat those they have personal relationships with very differently from strangers. When you've played football with someone on a Thursday, you're more likely to share useful information with them on a Friday, more likely to flag a mistake before it becomes a problem, and more likely to assume good faith when something goes wrong.

The sports hour is also a nudge in disguise. It gets enrolled as a default slot in everyone's weekly calendar. You can opt out, but most people won't, because it's fun and social. Over time, the informal bonds it builds do real organisational work — reducing friction, increasing psychological safety, and making the whole environment one where cooperation feels natural rather than strategic.

Why it actually works
Regular shared activity builds genuine trust between colleagues across departments
Physical activity reduces stress and improves mood, which carries into the working day
People help coworkers they know personally — sport creates that familiarity quickly
Default calendar slot means high participation without any mandate or pressure
Rotating sports and mixed teams each week prevent cliques from forming

Productivity up. Cooperation down.

A government agency rewarded individual output. Output went up. Then everything else went down.

§ IV · Governance and Welfare

So What Does This Actually Mean?

People's Wellbeing

Systems that only track output slowly eat away at what makes work meaningful. When trust goes, you get turnover, sick days, and work that technically gets done but stops being any good.

Governance

Nudge tools like smart defaults improve both efficiency and wellbeing at once — and unlike mandates, they preserve freedom of choice, which makes them easier to defend politically.

"The question is never whether to architect the choice environment. It already is. The question is whether to do it thoughtfully."

Thaler and Sunstein, Nudge (2008)

People Don't Think Like Economists

Most employees follow defaults, copy colleagues, and act on instinct. Designing systems that assume perfectly rational agents will keep producing surprises.

Use Both Tools Together

Financial incentives plus behavioural design. Clear structures give direction; smart defaults make cooperation the easier option. The goal is to make the good choice easy, not to trick anyone.

§ V · Real World

This Actually Happened. At Microsoft.

Big Tech Case
Microsoft
Stack Ranking Era · Early 2000s to 2013
What went wrong
The system that turned teammates into rivals

For over a decade, Microsoft ranked every team on a forced bell curve: top 20%, middle 70%, bottom 10%. The bottom group faced demotion or firing — regardless of actual performance.

Since only a fixed number could be top-ranked, working alongside a talented colleague became a threat. People hoarded information and avoided high-performers so they wouldn't look worse by comparison.

"People responsible for features will openly sabotage other people's efforts. I learned to give the appearance of being courteous while withholding just enough to ensure colleagues didn't get ahead of me."
Former Microsoft developer, Vanity Fair
Crowding-Out Effect Zero-Sum Competition Social Preferences Destroyed
What they did about it
Satya Nadella scrapped it and built something very different

In 2013, Microsoft abolished stack ranking. Under Nadella, the company shifted to continuous feedback, team-based goals, and a culture built around a "growth mindset." No more forced curves.

Employee satisfaction jumped from a Glassdoor score of 3.7 in 2013 to 4.4 by 2023. Azure became a cloud giant. The company went from a "lost decade" to a multi-trillion dollar resurgence.

A near-perfect real-world mirror of the case study: individual incentives crushed cooperation, and switching to collaborative structures brought it back.

§ VI · Proposed Solutions

Two Ideas That Could Actually Fix This

Now we know what goes wrong and why. The real question: what do you actually do about it? These two solutions go beyond tweaking bonuses and address the root problem — people need to feel part of something, not competing within it.

01
Solution One
A Game-Based Work System

Take what makes games fun — points, levels, team quests — and wire them into how everyday work gets recognised. Not to make work feel like a video game, but to make effort visible, collaboration rewarded, and progress something people can actually see.

In a traditional system, quietly helping a colleague doesn't show up in your review. In a game-based system, it does. You earn points for sharing knowledge, mentoring, or finishing a cross-team project. Teams accumulate points together and unlock shared rewards.

The rewards are transparent, frequent, and tied to behaviours the organisation actually wants — not just what's easiest to measure. And crucially, it's cooperative by default: nobody loses points because a colleague did well.

How it works in practice
Points for collaboration, knowledge-sharing, and team milestones
Contributions are visible to everyone across the organisation
Teams level up together and unlock shared perks — not cash prizes
Monthly challenges rotate to keep things fresh and prevent gaming
No forced rankings. No zero-sum competition. Everyone can win.

02
Solution Two
A Weekly Sports Hour for Team Building

One hour a week. Everyone stops working and plays together. It sounds simple, but the behavioural economics behind it is solid. Shared physical activity builds real trust — because it's regular, low-pressure, and fun in a way no Saturday workshop ever quite manages.

When you've only ever interacted with a colleague through tasks, your mental model of them is built on outcomes alone. A weekly sports session breaks that frame. You become people who played a game together — and research shows you'll share more information, catch each other's mistakes earlier, and assume good faith more readily.

It's also a nudge in disguise. The session is a default calendar slot. You can opt out, but most people won't. Over time, the informal bonds it builds reduce friction, increase psychological safety, and make cooperation feel natural rather than strategic.

Why it actually works
Shared activity builds genuine trust across departments
Physical activity reduces stress and carries into the working day
People help coworkers they know personally — sport creates that fast
Default calendar slot means high participation with no pressure
Rotating sports and mixed teams each week prevent clique formation
